What is a Sentence?

Basic Definition:

A sentence is a complete set of words that conveys meaning.

A sentence MUST have:

  • a subject (who or what the sentence is about)
  • a verb (action or state of being)
  • a complete idea (makes sense on its own)

A sentence MUST:

  • begin with a capital letter
  • end with punctuation (. ! ?)

‚úÖ Complete Sentences:

I smiled.

The students studied for the test.

She is a talented musician.

‚ùå NOT Complete Sentences:

I like. (Missing: what do you like? Incomplete idea)

Running in the park. (Missing: who is running? No subject)

The tall building. (Missing: verb - what about the building?)

What is a Simple Sentence?

Definition:

A simple sentence contains ONE independent clause with ONE subject-verb combination expressing ONE complete thought.

Independent Clause: A group of words that has a subject and a verb and expresses a complete thought. It can stand alone as a sentence.

Important: Simple sentences can be quite long. They can have two or more subjects, two or more verbs, or multiple phrases, but they still express just ONE main idea.

Examples of Simple Sentences:

Basic: I like vanilla ice cream.

With compound subject: Ice cream and gelato are popular desserts.

With compound verb: The ice cream melted and turned sour.

With added phrases: On hot days throughout summer, many people like to eat different brands and flavors of ice cream.

üí° Key Point: Even with added words and phrases, if there's only ONE subject-verb relationship expressing ONE complete thought, it's still a simple sentence!

What FANBOYS Connect in Simple Sentences

Coordinating conjunctions can join:

Nouns: Would you like toast or cereal for breakfast?

Verbs: The children ran and jumped all over the playground.

Adjectives: The old castle seemed grand yet mysterious.

Phrases: Ali is saving money to pay for his education and to buy a home.

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Rule: When FANBOYS join words or phrases (NOT complete sentences) in a simple sentence, generally NO comma is needed before the conjunction!

Punctuation Rules for Simple Sentences

Rule 1: Conjunctions Joining Two Items

When joining TWO nouns, verbs, adjectives, or phrases with FANBOYS: NO comma

‚úÖ Correct: Ice cream and gelato are popular desserts.

‚úÖ Correct: The ice cream melted and turned sour.

‚ùå Incorrect: Ice cream, and gelato are popular desserts.

Rule 2: Lists of Three or More Items

When listing THREE or more items: use commas between items, including before the final conjunction (Oxford comma)

‚úÖ Correct: I like chocolate, vanilla, and strawberry-flavored ice cream.

‚úÖ Correct: The children ran, jumped, and played on the playground.

‚ùå Incorrect: I like chocolate vanilla and strawberry-flavored ice cream.

Note: British English sometimes omits the comma before "and" in lists, but American academic English typically includes it.

Rule 3: Introductory Phrases

Use a comma AFTER introductory phrases or words at the beginning of a sentence

‚úÖ Correct: In summer, sales at Baskin Robbins significantly increase.

‚úÖ Correct: Ideally, ice cream should be kept at 0 degrees Fahrenheit.

‚úÖ Correct: On hot days throughout summer, many people eat ice cream.

‚ùå Incorrect: In summer sales at Baskin Robbins significantly increase.

However: When the phrase comes at the END, usually NO comma:

Sales at Baskin Robbins significantly increase in summer.

Rule 4: Transitional Expressions

Use commas to set off expressions like "such as," "especially," "for example," "including"

‚úÖ Correct: I like ice cream, such as Baskin Robbins and Haagen-Dazs.

‚úÖ Correct: I like ice cream, especially Baskin Robbins and Haagen-Dazs.

‚úÖ Correct: In summer, especially June and July, sales for ice cream increase.

üìù Key Takeaways

  • Simple sentences have ONE subject-verb relationship expressing ONE complete thought
  • Must have a subject, verb, and complete idea
  • Begin with a capital letter and end with punctuation
  • FANBOYS conjunctions join equal parts (nouns, verbs, phrases) - no comma with two items
  • Use commas in lists of 3+ items (including before final "and")
  • Use comma after introductory phrases at the start of sentences
  • Use commas to set off expressions like "such as," "especially," "for example"
  • In academic writing, avoid starting with "And," "But," or "So" (except "Yet")
üéØ Practice Tips:
1. Read your sentences out loud to check if they're complete
2. Identify the subject and verb - every sentence needs both!
3. Count items in lists - 2 items = no comma before "and"; 3+ items = commas needed
4. Look for introductory words/phrases and add commas after them
5. Simple doesn't mean short - sentences can be long and still be simple!
