What is a Compound Sentence?

Definition:

A compound sentence is made by joining two independent clauses (complete sentences) with a coordinating conjunction.

Independent Clause: A group of words that has a subject and a verb and expresses a complete thought. It can stand alone as a sentence.

Examples:

I like ice cream but Ali prefers gelato.

The movie was boring, yet I watched all of it.

She studied hard, so she passed the exam.

üí° Important: Both parts of a compound sentence must be complete sentences that can stand alone. Each must have its own subject and verb! Also, think carefully about which conjunction creates a logical relationship between your clauses.

Punctuation Rules

Rule: Comma + Coordinating Conjunction

When joining two independent clauses with a FANBOYS conjunction, place a comma before the conjunction.

‚úÖ Correct: I wanted to go outside, but it started raining.

‚ùå Incorrect: I wanted to go outside but it started raining. (missing comma)

Exception: If both clauses are very short (usually 5 words or less), you may omit the comma.

Example: I like ice cream but Ali prefers gelato.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistake: Don't confuse compound sentences with simple sentences that have compound verbs!

Simple sentence with compound verb: Ice cream melted and turned sour. (No comma - same subject)
Compound sentence: The ice cream melted, and it turned sour. (Comma needed - two subjects)

When to Use Compound Sentences

Use compound sentences to:

  • Show relationships between related ideas
  • Add variety to your writing (avoid too many short sentences)
  • Connect equal ideas that are related to each other
  • Make your writing flow more smoothly
  • Show contrast, cause-effect, or choice between ideas

Compare these:

Choppy (All simple sentences):
The weather was bad. I went to the park. I wanted to exercise.

Better (Mix of simple and compound):
The weather was bad, but I went to the park because I wanted to exercise.

üìù Key Takeaways

  • Compound sentences join two independent clauses with a FANBOYS conjunction
  • Remember: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So
  • Always use a comma before the FANBOYS when joining independent clauses
  • Both clauses must have a subject and verb and express complete thoughts
  • Use compound sentences to add variety and flow to your writing
  • In academic writing, avoid starting sentences with FANBOYS (except "Yet")
üéØ Practice Tips:
1. Read your sentences out loud to check if they sound natural
2. Make sure each part could stand alone as a complete sentence
3. Choose the FANBOYS that best shows the relationship between your ideas
4. Don't overuse compound sentences - mix them with simple and complex sentences for best results!
